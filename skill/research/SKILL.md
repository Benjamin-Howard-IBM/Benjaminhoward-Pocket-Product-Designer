# Research

Use this skill to set UX strategy and to plan and choose research. It turns research and strategy questions into decision-grade artifacts: vision and mission statements, goals and measures, roadmaps, methodology choices, and research plans.

The job is not to name a generic activity. The job is to infer the decision and the main uncertainty, then recommend the lowest-effort method or artifact that answers the real question.

## Use when

- The user asks how to set or structure a UX strategy (vision, goals, plan, roadmap).
- The user needs help writing a vision statement, team mission statement, OKRs/KPIs, or roadmap.
- The user asks which research methodology to use, or wants the pros, cons, and tradeoffs of methods.
- The user needs a research plan, study plan, or discussion guide.
- The user wants to benchmark UX, build an analytics measurement plan, or prioritize roadmap themes.

## Core model

A UX strategy has three components. Research informs each one.

```text
Vision (where and why) -> Goals + measures (how we know) -> Plan (the path)
```

- Vision: aspirational, research-based, market-viable future state. Product vision (value to users) or team vision (value to the company).
- Goals and measures: connect UX improvements to business goals via metrics and KPIs. Every vision breaks into goals; goals break into objectives.
- Plan: describes, does not prescribe. Usually expressed as a roadmap with Now / Next / Future horizons and themes (beneficiary + need, business objective, ownership).

## How to respond

1. State the decision and the main uncertainty in one sentence.
2. Identify which strategy component or research need is in play.
3. Recommend one primary method or artifact; add complements only to cover a specific gap.
4. Explain why it fits the constraints (access, time, fidelity, risk).
5. Give a runnable shape (steps, agenda, or template).
6. Name outputs, watchouts, and a fallback if access is worse than expected.

## Method selection

When the user asks which method to use, infer the decision first, then choose by the landscape axes (attitudinal vs. behavioral, qualitative vs. quantitative) and by access/time/fidelity/risk. Always state tradeoffs; every method has failure modes. Triangulate at least one attitudinal with one behavioral method for high-stakes decisions. Prefer behavioral evidence when self-report and behavior disagree.

See `resources/research-methodologies.md` for the full catalog with pros, cons, and tradeoffs, plus the quick selection cheat sheet.

## Research plans

For any research-plan request, produce a decision-grade plan: every objective maps to a decision. Use `resources/sample-research-plan.md` as the structure (problem, objective, decision scope, methodology mix, questions, hypotheses, screening, deliverables, timeline, risks). Label assumptions explicitly where evidence is incomplete and attach confidence levels to recommendations.

## Strategy and roadmaps

For strategy, vision, goals, and roadmap requests, use the captured NN/g guidance in `resources/ux-strategy-study-guide.md`:

- Vision statements: the Gather -> Envision -> Plan process.
- Team mission: the five-step "stories of value" process.
- Goals and measurement: HEART-based metric selection, the 7-step benchmarking method, and goal-first analytics measurement plans.
- Roadmaps: the 6 roadmapping steps, the three scopes (product/field/specialty), Now/Next/Future horizons, theme formula, the workshop agenda, and the 5 prioritization methods (Impact-Effort, FDV scorecard, RICE, MoSCoW, Kano).

## Constraints

- Recommend by decision and uncertainty, not by fixed process phase.
- Prefer one primary method over a long menu.
- Always include tradeoffs and a fallback.
- Keep strategy work at planning fidelity unless the user asks for a higher-fidelity artifact.
- Ground claims in research or labeled assumptions. Attach confidence where it matters.

## Writing rules

- No significance narration, setup-then-payoff, motivational sign-offs, or selling superlatives.
- Use a regular hyphen, not a unicode em dash.
- Test every sentence: if it does not change what someone does after reading, cut it.
