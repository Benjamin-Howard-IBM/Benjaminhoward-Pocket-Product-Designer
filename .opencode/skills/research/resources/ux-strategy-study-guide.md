# NN/g UX Strategy Study Guide - Captured Reference

Source index: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/strategy-study-guide/

This file captures deep takeaways from the article links in Nielsen Norman Group's "UX Strategy: Study Guide." It is organized using the guide's own four sections: Overview, Vision, Goal Setting and Measurement, and Planning and Roadmapping. Video-only resources are listed with the publisher's description and a note that spoken content was not transcribed.

The core model the guide teaches:

```text
UX strategy = Vision (where and why) -> Goals + measures (how we know) -> Plan (the path)
```

A UX strategy is a plan of actions designed to reach an improved future state of the organization's user experience over an established period. Research informs each component so the strategy stays plausible and viable.

---

## 1. Overview

### UX Strategy: Definition and Components
Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-strategy/

**Summary:** A UX strategy is a 3-part plan (vision, goals, plan) that builds shared understanding of direction before designing and implementing solutions. It intentionally guides prioritization and execution of UX work over time, and it only works when practitioners can articulate how executing it makes the business more successful.

**Key points:**
- Definition: a plan of actions designed to reach an improved future state of the organization's user experience over an established period of time. Scope can be a single product/feature, multiple products, or an entire organization; the definition and components stay the same regardless of scope.
- Component 1, Vision or statement(s) of intent: the summary of the product/service in its ideal form and the value it provides. Must be strategically positioned (research-based and market-viable). Strategic intent emerges from either solving a few key problems for many users, or supporting broad needs of many customers in a narrow market.
- Component 2, Goals and measures: connect UX improvements to business goals via metrics and KPIs. UX must understand how the organization makes and spends money, or its goals look irrelevant. Goals are usually set by leadership; key results are set by teams (from a baseline) or inherited. Every vision breaks into multiple goals tied to end-user needs.
- Component 3, Plan: each goal breaks into objectives (actions/steps). A plan also includes focus areas, approximate timing, prerequisites/dependencies, and constituents to include. "Plans describe, not prescribe" - they communicate what and when, not how to research or which solution to build. Time horizon mirrors the scope of the vision (3 months to 2 years).
- Supporting factors for execution (DesignOps): People (team allocation, budgets), Process (consistent research/design/dev practices, governance, adaptation), and Tools/standards (design systems, brand expression, backlogs). DesignOps orchestrates these to scale design's impact.
- Risks of weak/no strategy: detachment from user needs, top-down decisions on business value alone, UX brought in only to clean up, arbitrary prioritization, chasing trends/competitor features, duplicate work, low morale, and difficulty advocating for UX headcount.
- Getting started questions: Why do we need a strategy? What actions bring the highest value? Who needs to be involved? How will we know we are successful (KPIs)?

**When to use / apply:** When setting mid- to long-term UX direction; when UX work feels reactive or disconnected from business priorities; when you need a shared artifact to align teams and stakeholders before committing design/dev resources.

## 2. Vision

A vision is the aspirational, future-state view of the experience users will have. It must be research-based and viable, and it has to be shared and visible to do any work.

### How to Create a UX-Vision Statement
Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-vision-statements/

**Summary:** A UX vision is an aspirational, future-state view of the experience people will have with a product, service, team, or organization. The article lays out a three-stage collaborative, research-backed process (Gather, Envision, Plan) to produce a shared vision that aligns teams and prioritizes UX work.

**Key points:**
- Vision vs. mission: a vision answers "What do we want to become in n years?"; a mission answers "What collective value do we generate now?" Both feed strategy and are checked against goals/objectives (metrics).
- Two vision types: product visions (impact/value a product provides users) and team visions (value a team provides the company). Use product vision for usability or direction problems; use team vision when others do not understand UX's value.
- Stage I - Gather: collect and distill existing research and stakeholder views before ideating. Methods: stakeholder interviews (buy-in), usability testing (even small studies), competitive analysis (fold strengths/weaknesses into the future state).
- Stage II - Envision: a collaborative workshop with three steps - share/review research, brainstorm and prioritize future-state attributes, craft the statement.
- "Obstacles and Opportunities" activity: present research themes; team reacts via color-coded dot-coding into Agree / Disagree / Surprising / Essential (multiple allowed). Mark individually on paper first to reduce groupthink. Dot-coding differs from dot-voting.
- Future-state attribute generation: use a focus question with a 2-5 year horizon, silent timed brainstorming on stickies, affinity diagramming, then dot voting (e.g., 5 red "must-have" dots at 2 pts each, 5 green "nice-to-have" dots at 1 pt each), tally to rank clusters.
- Crafting the statement: individuals draft statements in a timed activity, share, optionally dot-vote strong phrases; a vision lead synthesizes the final statement.
- Stage III - Plan: close the current-to-future gap with "Requisite Roundup" - brainstorm needs across Processes, People, Tools and tech, and Governance, then sequence required items into a draft timeline/roadmap.
- Communicate via a fit-for-culture artifact (document, video, vision board, prototype). Three guidelines: ground it in research, be inclusive, share it out. Requires a strong, trusted facilitator.

**When to use / apply:** When a team cannot articulate the value of its work; when there are severe usability issues or direction disagreement (product vision) or others do not understand UX (team vision); during rapid growth or cultural change.

### UX Team Mission Statement
Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-team-mission-statement/

**Summary:** A team mission statement is a concise articulation of a team's core purpose and the value it currently provides. The article gives a five-step collaborative process that derives the statement from concrete "stories of value," then guidelines for formalizing and using it.

**Key points:**
- Mission ("who we are and how we provide value now") vs. vision ("what we want to become in n years"). Mission, vision, strategy, and goals form a chain; team missions support higher-level visions.
- Use when a team lacks alignment on its value, cannot prioritize, is misunderstood by the org, or is at a growth inflection point.
- Step 1 - Introduce mission statements: align on the definition, share examples, surface challenges using "Start, Stop, Continue" (red = stop, green = start, yellow = continue), then postup, affinity, dot voting.
- Step 2 - Share stories of value: a concise narrative of a specific incident when the team's value was realized. Prompt: "What does it look like when we deliver our full value?" (use "What would it look like..." for nascent teams).
- Step 3 - Identify critical elements: reread stories in 3 rounds, each with a different mark, to find (1) people/groups supported, (2) actions taken, (3) changes/results produced.
- Step 4 - Cluster elements onto 3 boards (one per category); assign sub-groups to compile and report each category's themes.
- Step 5 - Draft statements: individuals generate many drafts within a time limit, share, discuss patterns; a lead synthesizes options for a vote.
- Final-statement guidelines: concise (ideally 10-15 words), simple, specific (should not fit any other team), and say it out loud to test memorability and natural phrasing.
- Uses: prioritize initiatives (a "flag in the sand"), market the team's value (standard elevator pitch), and grow the team strategically. Examples: Salesforce "Make work awesome, and make awesome work"; Home Depot "Help drive developer efficiency and UI consistency."

**When to use / apply:** When a team needs internal alignment and external recognition of its focus; when members struggle to prioritize varied work; when scaling influence or UX maturity.

### Brand Vocabulary in the Context of UX
Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-brand-terms-defined/

**Summary:** The article defines a confused set of brand terms and maps each to its role in UX, arguing a shared brand vocabulary is a prerequisite for translating brand into consistent user interactions. It frames a six-element brand framework teams must understand to make cohesive cross-channel decisions.

**Key points:**
- Brand (capital B) is a promise about what a company stands for or the value it offers; branding (lowercase) is the controlled manifestation through logos, guidelines, packaging. "Brand" is often misused to mean a product name or logo.
- Six core elements: (1) brand promise, (2) brand personality, (3) brand attributes, (4) mission, (5) vision, (6) brand principles.
- Brand promise: the unique, enduring reason for being; sometimes the tagline (Apple "Think different," FedEx "The world on time," Target "Expect more. Pay less."). In UX, use it as a metric - compare the experience against the promise before release.
- Brand personality: defined traits that personify the brand ("If the brand were a person, how would it act?"). Drives interaction, visual, and copy decisions.
- Brand attributes: the 3-7 characteristics that make up the personality (Walmart: Caring, Authentic, Innovative, Straightforward, Optimistic; Spotify: easy, personal, fun). Used as a design litmus test for flows and copy.
- Vision (why, ideal-world state) and mission (how we get there) express the promise tactically and help prioritize, roadmap, and scope (an MVP must satisfy part of the mission).
- Brand principles: a belief system of core values extending mission/vision; used as tie-breaking criteria between otherwise equal alternatives and as "bumpers" keeping work on-brand.
- Core argument: investing early in shared brand vocabulary prevents inconsistent understanding and a fractured experience.

**When to use / apply:** When translating brand/style guides into consistent experiences; when establishing a shared brand foundation across siloed teams; when using brand elements as decision tools (promise as release metric, attributes as litmus test, principles as tie-breakers).

## 3. Goal Setting and Measurement

Goals, metrics, and KPIs connect UX improvements to business goals. Define the goal first, then choose the measure.

### 7 Steps to Benchmark Your Product's UX
Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/product-ux-benchmarks/

**Summary:** UX benchmarking evaluates a product's experience using metrics measured against a meaningful standard (an earlier version, a competitor, an industry benchmark, or a stakeholder goal). It is a summative evaluation run at the end of a design cycle and works best as a repeated program with a fixed methodology so results stay comparable.

**Key points:**
- Seven steps: (1) choose what to measure, (2) decide how to measure, (3) collect a baseline, (4) redesign, (5) collect another measurement, (6) interpret, (7) optionally calculate ROI. After the first pass, future cycles restart at step 4.
- Step 1: define study context (product, user group, tasks), identify 5-10 top tasks via prioritization (e.g., dot voting), then select 2-4 metrics covering different UX aspects for long-term tracking.
- Metric selection adapts Google's HEART: Happiness (satisfaction, ease-of-use, NPS), Engagement (avg time on task, feature usage, conversion), Adoption (new accounts, sales, conversion), Retention (returning users, churn, renewal), and Task effectiveness/efficiency (errors, success rate, time on task).
- Context flips a metric's meaning: time on task should be high for engagement (reading) but low for efficiency (checkout). Align metrics to organizational KPIs (e.g., support-call cost).
- Step 2 methods: quantitative usability testing (task metrics), analytics (usage data, abandonment, adoption), surveys (self-reported ease, satisfaction, NPS). Ideally pair a self-report method with a behavioral one. Reuse existing org data when possible.
- Step 3: run and discard a pilot, account for external confounds (campaigns, economy); a single measurement is meaningless alone - compare to competitor, industry benchmark, or stakeholder goal.
- Step 5: let users adapt before re-measuring (about 2-3 weeks for daily-use products, 4-5 weeks for weekly-use); analytics allows continuous measurement.
- Step 6: use statistics to separate real change from noise. Worked example: time on task flat (6.28 to 6.32 min), success up (70% to 95%), SEQ up (5.4 to 6.2 on 1-7) indicates a successful redesign once differences are significant.
- Step 7 (optional): calculate ROI by tying a UX metric to a KPI (profit, cost, productivity, satisfaction). Present with a story, qualitative quotes, and documented assumptions/confounds.

**When to use / apply:** When tracking overall UX progress across releases; when comparing against a prior version, competitor, benchmark, or goal; when demonstrating UX value/ROI.

### Translating UX Goals into Analytics Measurement Plans
Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-goals-analytics/

**Summary:** Analytics should be driven by clearly defined UX goals, not by whatever metrics are easy to capture. Work top-down from goals to behavioral signals to specific metrics, keeping user outcomes (saving time, easing tasks) at the center.

**Key points:**
- Extends "focus on outcomes, not features" into analytics: defining the true goal is a prerequisite to choosing a meaningful measure.
- Use the repeated-"why?" technique to reach root causes. Example: "count plays" becomes percentage of unique plays per unique page view (discoverability) and contact-form completion rate of video watchers vs. non-watchers (effectiveness).
- Common mistake: defining a goal in terms of a metric. The real answer to "why" is never "to see the data." Analytics is a means to insight, not an end.
- Recommended sequence (top-down): (1) state the goal and the UX you want to ensure, (2) identify user behaviors that signal the goal is met, (3) narrow to the strongest signal(s), (4) define concrete measures.
- Bounce-rate example reframes "reduce bounce rate" into engaging, helpful content reaching the right audience; cues include widget interaction, photo zoom, reading to the bottom, or returning later.
- Example measures: scroll heatmaps (reading depth), event tracking (in-page interactions), referral sources/search keywords (audience fit), return-visit rate/frequency/recency (loyalty).
- For timesaving features, measure whether time is actually saved (decreased time on task, higher completion, fewer errors), not just adoption counts.
- Surface metrics like clicks or page views misrepresent success; good design is not about more clicks.

**When to use / apply:** When building or auditing an analytics measurement plan for a feature, page, or redesign; when connecting tracking to genuine UX outcomes or justifying usability ROI.

## 4. Planning and Roadmapping

A plan achieves each goal; every goal breaks into objectives. Roadmaps are the common artifact for packaging the plan.

### UX Roadmaps 101 (article + video)
Article source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-roadmaps/
Video source: https://www.nngroup.com/videos/ux-roadmaps-101/ (video content not transcribed)

**Summary:** A UX roadmap is a strategic, living artifact that aligns, prioritizes, and communicates a UX team's future work and the problems it intends to solve. It is the team's "North Star" and single source of truth, distinct from execution-tracking documents like release plans or Kanban boards.

**Key points:**
- Forms vary (lists, spreadsheets, decks, sticky walls) but all share structure across two dimensions: context (scope + time) and theme.
- Context = Scope (title, owner, date, high-level goals/vision) + Time across three horizons: Now (in-progress, well-defined), Next (near-future), Future (6+ months out, ambiguous).
- Themes plot into horizons; each theme uses a 3-part formula: (1) beneficiary and need, (2) business objective(s)/success metrics, (3) ownership (who and what high-level work).
- Secondary components: Completed and Future++ horizons, product areas, subthemes (common in Now), confidence estimates (e.g., 1-7 scale), and disclaimers (risks/requirements that scale with audience size).
- Adapt by scope (single initiative vs. portfolio; narrower scope = more concrete, shorter horizons) and lens (by discipline vs. by product team).
- Distinctions: roadmaps answer "what should we solve?" while release plans answer "how should we solve it?"; roadmaps are strategic, Kanban/project plans are execution; backlogs are ranked dev to-do lists; journey maps map user interaction, not future team work.
- Successful roadmaps are research-based, user-focused (outcomes over outputs), contextually appropriate, and collaborative/living.
- Concept originates from Motorola's 1987 technology-roadmap process.

**When to use / apply:** When a UX team needs to align and communicate a prioritized strategic direction; when separating "what to solve" from execution artifacts.

### 3 Types of Roadmaps (article + video)
Article source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/roadmap-types/
Video source: https://www.nngroup.com/videos/3-types-roadmaps/ (video content not transcribed)

**Summary:** Roadmaps involving UX work have three nesting scopes - product, field, and specialty - from broadest to narrowest. Each has distinct owners, benefits, and challenges; choose by the primary goal of the effort.

**Key points:**
- Product roadmaps: broadest, all future problems across functions (UX, marketing, content, research, dev, support, ops). Field roadmaps: all UX-area problems only. Specialty roadmaps: a single UX area (e.g., user research).
- Scopes nest: product informs field, which expands UX-owned themes; specialty expands a single area's themes.
- Product roadmaps owned by product managers (UX lead may step in when no PM, no bandwidth, or no user-centric mentality). Benefits: crossfunctional collaboration, shared mental model, breaking silos. Challenges: conflicting departmental goals, politics, laborious.
- Field roadmaps owned by UX directors/leads or UX program managers, can span products. Benefits: bridge UX areas, communicate the UX process. Challenges: too broad for meaningful resource allocation; must coordinate with the product roadmap.
- Specialty roadmaps owned by lead/senior practitioners, ResearchOps/DesignOps, or individual contributors. Benefits: communicate bandwidth/allocation, unite an area, easy to create with little politics (good first roadmap). Challenge: limited audience; keep time-boxed and low-effort.

**When to use / apply:** Choose scope by the primary goal - product for crossfunctional vision, field to convey the UX process, specialty to advocate resources and balance bandwidth.

### The 6 Steps to UX Roadmapping (article + video)
Article source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/roadmapping-steps/
Video source: https://www.nngroup.com/videos/ux-roadmaps-6-steps/ (video content not transcribed)

**Summary:** Roadmapping follows six steps that gather and cluster inputs into themes, then prioritize and visualize them. It applies to any scope or industry and treats the roadmap as a living artifact.

**Key points:**
- Six steps: (1) Establish Goals, (2) Gather Inputs, (3) Create Themes, (4) Prioritize Themes, (5) Visualize and Share, (6) Revisit and Update.
- Step 1 - three parts: Why (single primary objective: awareness, alignment, UX-process education, bandwidth management, prioritization, vision, or MVP), What (scope: product/discipline/specialty and which products), Who (crossdisciplinary core team + stakeholder buy-in).
- Step 2 - gather artifacts (prior roadmaps, product roadmaps, journey maps, service blueprints, leadership strategy, backlogs) and data (qual/quant research, support logs, market surveys, voice-of-customer); fill gaps with stakeholder interviews.
- Step 3 - mirrors thematic analysis: 3A inventory candidate problems, 3B cluster via affinity diagrams with labels, 3C polish each cluster into a theme (beneficiary, need, business objective).
- Step 4 - prioritization methods: RICE, critical path, Kano, ROI scorecard, MoSCoW (custom allowed). Sub-steps: 4A criteria (4-8 factors; start with 2 user + 2 business), 4B scale (e.g., 1-3), 4C score and force-rank (optional weighting).
- User criteria examples: impact, % users impacted, severity, alternatives available. Business criteria: strategy alignment, differentiation, ROI, effort (time + cost).
- After ranking, top themes go to Now/Next, lower to Future/Future++, then discuss to buy-in.
- Step 5 - match fidelity to audience; stakeholder-facing roadmaps are higher fidelity with context, date, version. Set context and request specific feedback when sharing.
- Step 6 - living artifact revisited at least monthly: move finished themes to Completed, update progressed themes, clarify future themes, save past versions.

**When to use / apply:** When you need a repeatable end-to-end procedure to build a roadmap from raw inputs through prioritization and sharing; when starting small and growing into a workshop.

### UX-Roadmapping Workshops: Agenda + Activities
Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/roadmapping-workshop/

**Summary:** Roadmaps must be created collaboratively to be adopted; a workshop is the mechanism, executing the theme-creation and prioritization steps of the broader process.

**Key points:**
- A workshop covers steps 3 (create themes) and 4 (prioritize); the facilitator handles goals and inputs before, and visualize/share/update after.
- Size scales with facilitator experience: 3-6 people if new, 6-12 for higher-level roadmaps if experienced.
- Pre-work: identify a single primary purpose; choose scope and assemble a crossdisciplinary core team; gather inputs; plan logistics (materials, notetaker/timekeeper/scribe roles, agenda, expectations).
- Core 3-hour agenda: Introduction (30 min: icebreaker + hopes & fears, 3 each), Readout (15 min: context + inputs), Distill Themes (45 min: individual inventory, then small-group converge into named low-fi themes), Break (15 min), Affinity Diagramming (30 min: cluster, then finalize each theme with name, beneficiary, need, business objective), Prioritization (45 min).
- Prioritization block: 4-8 agreed criteria (mix user/business) with a scale (e.g., 1-3); each member rates every theme; average to force a rank; play back top themes into nearest horizons for buy-in.
- Keep output low-fidelity in the workshop; make high-fidelity afterward.
- Alternative two-workshop approach: a 1.5-hour preliminary workshop (goals + inputs, with embedded stakeholder interviews) then a 2.5-hour second workshop (straight to distilling themes, affinity, prioritization). Suits orgs new to roadmapping.
- Post-workshop: visualize/polish in a shared tool, share recap + next steps, schedule a follow-up about a month later.

**When to use / apply:** When you need a roadmap stakeholders will adopt; when goals/inputs are ready and you need to create and prioritize themes; when educating stakeholders on the UX process.

### 5 Prioritization Methods in UX Roadmapping
Source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/prioritization-methods/

**Summary:** Prioritization methods base roadmap decisions on objective, relevant criteria rather than opinion. No method is inherently better; choose by project context, team culture, and success criteria.

**Key points:**
- Impact-Effort Matrix: plots user value (impact) vs. implementation complexity (effort) into four quadrants - quick wins (low effort/high impact), big bets (high effort/high value), money pits (high effort/low impact), fill-ins (low effort/low impact). Often dot-voted by domain (designers vote impact, developers vote effort). Best for quick, democratic prioritization.
- Feasibility, Desirability, Viability Scorecard (IDEO): sum numeric scores (e.g., 1-10) across feasibility (can it be built), desirability (do users want it), viability (does it sustain the business), then force-rank by total. Columns can swap (Dave Gray's NUF Test: New/Useful/Feasible) or be weighted. Best for custom criteria.
- RICE (Intercom): (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort. Reach = users affected per period (from analytics), Impact ~0.25-3, Confidence 25-100%, Effort in person-months. Best for technical teams comfortable with spreadsheets and many items.
- MoSCoW (Dai Clegg): Must Have, Should Have, Could Have, Will Not Have, via weighted dot voting; allocate resources starting with Must Haves. Best for clearly time-boxed work (without a time box everything inflates into Must Have).
- Kano Model (Noriaki Kano, 1984): score functionality (-2 to 2) and satisfaction (-2 to 2), plot into Attractive/Excitement, Performance, Indifferent, Must-be. Priority order: Performance, Must-be, Attractive, Indifferent. Best for forcing user-centric prioritization (satisfaction scores from user research).
- Methods can prioritize research questions, segments, features, ideas, or tasks; axes/criteria/weights are adaptable and combinable.

**When to use / apply:** When placing problems into the nearest roadmap horizons with a defensible rationale; match method to context (impact-effort for speed, scorecard for custom criteria, RICE for many items, MoSCoW for time-boxed work, Kano for user-centricity); choose a method the team will adopt.

### UX Roadmaps: Who, When, and How Much Time? (article + video)
Article source: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-roadmaps-faq/
Video source: https://www.nngroup.com/videos/ux-roadmaps-common-questions/ (video content not transcribed)

**Summary:** Answers common practitioner questions about how roadmaps differ from project plans, who owns them, when they are made, and how much time they take. Alignment is the main benefit.

**Key points:**
- Roadmaps vs. project plans: roadmaps are strategic, vision-oriented (the what and why); project plans are execution-focused lists of discrete tasks (the how). The roadmap bridges vision and project tracking.
- Three scopes: product (all functions), field (all UX areas, can span products), specialty (one UX area). Field roadmaps are most common in UX; specialty roadmaps are most common in UX research.
- Benefits split: process benefits (align/prioritize requests into themes, common vocabulary, buy-in via participation) and artifact benefits (single source of truth bridging disciplines, communicate the UX process to lower-maturity stakeholders).
- Four moments to create a roadmap: a new initiative, when something has gone awry, a leadership change, and annual/quarterly planning (most common).
- Ownership by scope: product (product managers, or UX leads cocreating), field (UX directors/leads or UX program managers), specialty (team leads or individuals, common in startups).
- Time to create: in a survey of 104 practitioners, the majority spend less than 1 day; about 34% spend more than a week (longer timelines include discovery research, vision, criteria, alignment - not the drawing itself).
- Update cadence: weekly to monthly; a recurring monthly meeting is recommended. Updating moves completed themes to Completed, shifts next themes into Now, adds insights as subthemes, reprioritizes, adds new themes to Future++. Save prior versions for communication and onboarding.
- Presenting: schedule a meeting and tell a story with progressive disclosure - set context (Who, Why, Recent Progress, Research), then reveal Now, Next, Future, then the full roadmap with open questions and next steps.

**When to use / apply:** When justifying roadmap scope, ownership, timing, or cadence; when choosing the right roadmap type for an audience; when planning how to present a roadmap to drive alignment.
